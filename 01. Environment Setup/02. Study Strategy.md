# Introduction

Preparing for the **eJPT** is not about memorizing hundreds of commands or exploits.

The certification is designed to evaluate your ability to think like a penetration tester, follow a structured methodology, and identify the correct attack path.

A good penetration tester succeeds because they understand **why** they are performing each step, not because they remember every command.

This repository is designed to help you build that mindset.

---

# Learning Philosophy

When studying penetration testing, always follow this order:

```text
Understand
      ↓
Practice
      ↓
Repeat
      ↓
Document
      ↓
Master
```

Avoid copying commands without understanding what they do.

Instead, ask yourself:

- Why am I running this command?
- What information am I trying to obtain?
- What should I do with the results?
- What is my next step?

Learning the methodology is far more valuable than memorizing syntax.

# Daily Study Routine

Consistency is more important than studying for long periods once a week.

A productive study session could look like this:

## Step 1 — Theory (20%)

Read the documentation.

Understand:

- Concepts
- Protocols
- Attack vectors
- Methodology

Do not rush this part.

---

## Step 2 — Practice (70%)

Immediately apply what you learned.

Examples:

- Scan machines
- Enumerate services
- Exploit vulnerabilities
- Transfer files
- Escalate privileges

Learning by doing is the fastest way to improve.

---

## Step 3 — Review (10%)

Before ending your session:

- Review your notes.
- Save useful commands.
- Document mistakes.
- Record new techniques.

# Hands-On Practice

Reading alone is not enough.

For every topic, complete at least one practical lab.

Good practice platforms include:

- TryHackMe
- Hack The Box
- VulnHub
- Metasploitable
- OWASP Broken Web Applications
- DVWA

Try to solve machines without reading walkthroughs.

If you become stuck:

1. Take a break.
2. Review your enumeration.
3. Read only the smallest hint necessary.

# Taking Notes

Professional penetration testers document everything.

Useful notes include:

- IP addresses
- Open ports
- Service versions
- Credentials
- Vulnerabilities
- Exploits used
- Reverse shell commands
- Privilege escalation vectors
- Interesting files
- Loot

Good notes save time and prevent repeating work.

# Building a Personal Cheatsheet

Do not rely only on public cheatsheets.

Create your own.

Your personal cheatsheet should contain:

- Nmap commands

- Reverse shells
- File transfer methods
- Linux privilege escalation commands
- Windows privilege escalation commands
- SMB enumeration
- SQL Injection payloads
- Gobuster commands
- Burp shortcuts

Keeping your own reference helps reinforce what you learn.

# Common Mistakes

Many beginners struggle because they:

- Memorize commands without understanding them.
- Ignore service enumeration.
- Skip note-taking.
- Jump directly into exploitation.
- Depend too much on Metasploit.
- Forget to verify results manually.
- Give up too quickly.
- Ignore failed commands instead of understanding why they failed.

Every failed attempt is an opportunity to learn.

---

# Final Week Before the Exam

The last week should focus on review, not learning new topics.

Recommended activities:

- Review your notes.
- Complete full penetration testing labs.
- Practice privilege escalation.
- Review common web vulnerabilities.
- Refresh your Nmap workflow.
- Test your personal cheatsheet.
- Solve one or two complete machines from start to finish.

The goal is to reinforce your methodology and improve your confidence.

---

# Exam Day Tips

Before starting the exam:

- Ensure you have a stable internet connection.
- Close unnecessary applications.
- Prepare a note-taking tool.
- Have your cheatsheet available.
- Stay hydrated.
- Take short breaks if needed.

During the exam:

- Enumerate first.
- Exploit second.
- Escalate privileges.
- Pivot only when necessary.
- Read every question carefully.
- Double-check your answers before submitting.

Remember:

> Enumeration solves more problems than exploitation.

Stay calm, work methodically, and trust your preparation.

- [ ] I can stabilize reverse shells.
- [ ] I can perform Linux privilege escalation.
- [ ] I can perform Windows privilege escalation.
- [ ] I understand pivoting techniques.
- [ ] I am comfortable documenting my findings.

If you can confidently check every box, you are well prepared for the eJPT.
